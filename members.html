What advice would you give for current students looking for jobs/internships?
Study hard and NETWORK! You can be an nerd all you want but in the IT world you need people skills as much as you need technical skills; maybe even more so. When did you graduate and what degree/certificate did you gain from it?

I graduated from Mt. Sac in 2018 with an A.S. in Computer Network Administration and Security Management.

What's your current profession?

I currently work as an IT/Cybersecurity Analyst

How did the CIS department/program helped prepare you for your profession or in general?

The CIS program was effective in preparing me for my current profession. When I first started the CIS program, I had no formal IT work experience or education. By the time I finished the program, I felt confident in the knowledge and skills that I had gained. The classes incorporated hands-on learning. Depending on your background, some of the classes can be quite challenging. The challenges I encountered certainly helped me grow quickly and solidified what I learned. Despite that IT/cybersecurity is a very broad field, the CIS program is focused enough to give you an appropriate baseline of knowledge. As part of my work, I analyze the IT environment of different organizations. Each organization has its own unique IT environment, yet I still use what I learned from the CIS program often. So, in any case, while there is so much to learn, the CIS program is effective in getting you started. Please note, to make the most of the program, it is necessary to participate in club activities and any competitions. Not participating in these extracurricular activities can be quite detrimental.

What advice would you give for current students looking for jobs/internships?

Volatile economic conditions, barriers to entry, or an otherwise very competitive job market can make finding a job in IT/cybersecurity very difficult and discouraging. The demand for years of work experience remains a top barrier to entry. However, please do not be discouraged, as it very much can be done. First, when applying, plan on submitting at least 30 applications before expecting to hear from potential employers. Please consider the many different types of potential employers. Government agency positions tend to be more entry-level friendly, and their interviews also tend to be less difficult. You need to search on specific sites like USA Jobs to find these positions. In general, many IT/cybersecurity-related jobs require you to meet certain eligibility requirements to obtain a security clearance. Please keep this in mind when searching for positions. In lieu of work experience, here are some ways to strengthen yourself as a candidate: If possible, get involved in relevant research projects/writing research papers, as well as presenting them. Participate in relevant clubs and note any leadership or events that you have helped organize for that club. If you have strong grades in certain relevant classes, list those grades. Include any special electives that you have taken. Feel free to note any freelance work you have done. Make your own website/online portfolio and include it in your resume. Joining organizations such as InfraGard, ISACA, etc. are also a plus. In addition, it often helps to demonstrate that you show care for others. For example, I volunteered at an organization for several years. The fact that I volunteered, and in conjunction with activities I did with the CIS club, resulted in it being a determining factor on whether I was awarded a scholarship at Cal Poly Pomona. I was awarded that scholarship, which ultimately helped lead to the job I have today. I fully recommended applying for internships. I did a summer internship in 2019. While I would have preferred to get more relevant experience out of my internship than I received, it was worthwhile overall. Internships give great networking opportunities and provide an opportunity to evaluate the organizational culture of a company. Often there is great potential to be rehired full-time, as I personally knew several interns who were rehired for full-time work. Federally Funded Research and Development Centers, such as the MITRE Corporation, or Sandia National Laboratories are great places to intern with. Finally, obtaining IT certifications help. There is much discussion on what certifications you should get, how many you should get, or how useful they are in the grand scheme of things. I have seen times when having a certification was useful and other times less so. Bottom line, I recommend obtaining entry-level certifications, such as Security+, Network+, etc. This is a good baseline. From that point, I would recommend not pursuing additional certifications unless you have a specific reason to do so, such as if a certain certification would help lead to you obtaining a certain job or would otherwise directly benefit you.
